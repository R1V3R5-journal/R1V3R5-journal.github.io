### Appendix S1: Terminal-Lake vs. Open-Lake Salinity Behavior

#### Salt-Balance Framework

For any lake, the dissolved-load balance is:

$$\frac{d(C \cdot S)}{dt} = C_{in} \cdot Q_{in} - C \cdot Q_{out},$$

where $C$ is lake solute concentration, $S$ is lake storage volume, $C_{in}$ is inflow concentration, and $Q_{out}$ is surface outflow. Precipitation is assumed dilute ($C_P \approx 0$).

To align with the main paper's notation, define the total dissolved-load export term

$$
Q_{exp} \equiv Q_{out} + Q_{sub} + Q_{warp},
$$

so the same balance can be written as

$$
\frac{d(C \cdot S)}{dt} = C_{in} \cdot Q_{in} - C \cdot Q_{exp}.
$$

This keeps the salt argument and the storage-balance argument on the same term structure.

#### Terminal Lake ($Q_{out} = 0$)

When there is no surface outlet and no other export pathway, the right-hand side reduces to $C_{in} \cdot Q_{in} \geq 0$, so $C \cdot S$ can only increase. If storage $S$ is approximately stable (maintained by evaporation), lake concentration $C$ must increase monotonically. Because evaporation removes water but not solutes, no restoring export feedback appears.

#### Open Lake ($Q_{out} > 0$)

With surface outflow, the salt balance at steady state ($d/dt = 0$) gives a finite equilibrium concentration:

$$C^* = \frac{C_{in} \cdot Q_{in}}{Q_{out}}.$$

If water balance holds without evaporation ($Q_{out} \approx Q_{in}$), then $C^* \approx C_{in}$: lake salinity tracks inflow salinity and stays fresh. Even with evaporative loss reducing $Q_{out}$ below $Q_{in}$, $Q_{in}/Q_{out}$ remains bounded, so $C^*$ stays finite. 

Higher concentration increases exported dissolved load per unit outflow, providing restoring feedback toward $C^*$.

The same argument applies when export is subsurface or warp-mediated rather than surface flow: any pathway carrying dissolved load at lake concentration can support a freshwater steady state.

#### Climate Forcing Affects Rate, Not Direction

Under **humid forcing** (high $P$, moderate $E$), a terminal lake still concentrates solutes, but more slowly because dilution is stronger. Under **semi-humid forcing** (moderate $P$, higher $E$), concentration accelerates. In both regimes, direction is unchanged: terminal basins salinize; open basins approach bounded steady state. Climate changes timescale, not outcome direction.

#### Implication for Lake Hylia

Lake Hylia receives sustained inflow and supports Zoras and fish species sensitive to water quality. These observations are inconsistent with long-run terminal-lake behavior under plausible forcing:

1. A purely evaporative terminal lake (H0) predicts progressive solute accumulation regardless of climate regime; freshwater persistence contradicts this.
2. Freshwater appearance is therefore independent evidence against terminal closure, separate from the storage-balance argument.
3. Only a configuration with at least one dissolved-load export pathway - surface ($Q_{out}$), subsurface ($Q_{sub}$), or warp-mediated ($Q_{warp}$) - supports a bounded freshwater steady state.

H4a (quality filtering without volumetric export) can improve apparent water quality but does not, by itself, supply the volumetric term needed for storage balance.

This appendix therefore provides a second diagnostic independent of the $\Delta S$ argument: persistent freshness requires an active export pathway.

### Appendix S2: Symbolic Sensitivity of Hidden Outflow Terms

Start from the working balance:

$$
\Delta S = P + Q_{in} - E - Q_{out} - Q_{sub} - Q_{warp}.
$$

Define the hidden-outflow aggregate and forcing gap:

$$
Q_h \equiv Q_{sub}+Q_{warp}, \qquad B \equiv P + Q_{in} - E - Q_{out}.
$$

Then:

$$
\Delta S = B - Q_h.
$$

Long-run stability requires $\Delta S \approx 0$, so the hidden outflow requirement is:

$$
Q_h^{req} = B.
$$

This yields three symbolic regimes:

1. If $B \le 0$, hidden outflow is not required for nonpositive storage trend ($Q_h^{req}=0$).
2. If $B > 0$, hidden outflow is required and must satisfy $Q_h \ge B$ for non-growth and $Q_h = B$ for steady storage.
3. If $Q_h < B$, then $\Delta S > 0$ and the lake expands over time.

For a fixed climate and mapped terms, the minimal sufficiency condition in $(Q_{sub}, Q_{warp})$ space is the half-plane:

$$
Q_{sub} + Q_{warp} \ge B,
$$

with equality as the minimum boundary. This immediately shows which hidden-outflow combinations are minimally sufficient:

- Subsurface-only sufficiency: $(Q_{sub},Q_{warp})=(B,0)$.
- Warp-only sufficiency: $(Q_{sub},Q_{warp})=(0,B)$.
- Mixed sufficiency: any pair with $Q_{sub}=\alpha B$, $Q_{warp}=(1-\alpha)B$, $0<\alpha<1$.

Sensitivity is linear:

$$
\frac{\partial \Delta S}{\partial Q_{sub}} = -1, \qquad
\frac{\partial \Delta S}{\partial Q_{warp}} = -1.
$$

So one unit increase in either hidden outflow term reduces storage tendency by the same amount. The data need is therefore not sensitivity estimation but partition identification: determining how much of $B$ is carried by $Q_{sub}$ versus $Q_{warp}$.

Under humid forcing, $B$ is typically smaller (higher $P$ but often lower evaporative demand relative to available water), so the minimally sufficient hidden-outflow boundary shifts toward the origin. Under semi-humid forcing, higher effective evaporative demand can either reduce or increase $B$ depending on concurrent changes in $P$, $Q_{in}$, and mapped $Q_{out}$; the criterion is unchanged, but the threshold magnitude $B$ shifts with forcing.

Result: when mapped terms leave $B>0$, long-run stability requires nonzero hidden outflow with feasible combinations on or above $Q_{sub}+Q_{warp}=B$.

### Appendix S3: Three Discriminating Predictions (In-Game Observables)

These predictions are designed to separate two competing regimes using only ALttP-observable constraints: **subsurface-dominant** hidden discharge ($Q_{sub} \gg Q_{warp}$) versus **warp-dominant** hidden discharge ($Q_{warp} \gg Q_{sub}$).

In this framing, H3 and H4b both fall under the warp-dominant branch because each contributes to $Q_{warp}$; H4a is excluded because it does not add volumetric discharge.

#### Prediction 1: Event-Gate Coupling Signature

If hidden discharge is warp-dominant, Lake Hylia state should show conditional coupling to world-state gates already known to affect magical water routing (for example, swamp watergate logic). Opening or closing a relevant gate should then produce a detectable change in Lake Hylia-adjacent water behavior (accessibility, connectivity, or persistence cues) within the same play session.

If hidden discharge is subsurface-dominant, Lake Hylia behavior should be comparatively insensitive to those event-gate toggles and remain stable across gate states.

Operational test: compare Lake Hylia-adjacent water cues before and after gate toggles while holding route and timing constant.

#### Prediction 2: Routing Topology Signature

If hidden discharge is warp-dominant, at least one Lake Hylia-adjacent whirlpool path should map to a net-export topology (destination outside the local recirculation loop, or lower-head sink with no compensating return path).

If hidden discharge is subsurface-dominant, whirlpool routing may remain closed-loop or bidirectional recirculation with zero net export, while long-run stability is still explained by non-warp leakage.

Operational test: frame-by-frame destination audit for all Lake Hylia-adjacent whirlpools, with each route labeled `net-export` or `recirculation`.

#### Prediction 3: Spatial Localization Signature

If hidden discharge is subsurface-dominant, strongest discharge indicators should localize near cave-bearing and sinkhole-like terrain around Lake Hylia margins (persistent local leakage analogs, not global network dependence).

If hidden discharge is warp-dominant, strongest indicators should localize at warp nodes and their network reachability structure, not at cave-adjacent terrain features.

Operational test: construct a binary indicator map over Lake Hylia tiles (`near-cave/sinkhole-like` vs `warp-node-connected`) and score which class better predicts observed hydraulic cues.

#### Decision Rule

Treat the mechanism as provisionally dominant if at least two of the three predictions are satisfied for that mechanism under repeated, route-controlled observations. If results split 1-1-1, classify as mixed regime and retain both terms with conservative bounds.

#### Aggregation Protocol

Apply a simple class-balance rule before drawing mechanism conclusions:

1. Require at least one supporting datum from `environment screen` class for any hydraulic-effect claim.
2. Cap `player-map representation` contributions at one independent vote per claim when entries are release-duplicate.
3. Treat `reconstruction` as supportive context unless independently corroborated by in-game observations.

This protocol prevents a single cartographic artifact from dominating inference and keeps ALttP-internal functional evidence as the primary discriminator.
